This site should not read like a pile of disconnected pages. It needs a small
grammar: what belongs in a note, what belongs in a project, and what should
eventually become a paper or manuscript.

The distinction I want is simple.

| Surface | Job | Evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Notes | Make thinking legible early | observations, sketches, implementation details |
| Projects | Show the artifact | code, traces, measurements, reproducible workflows |
| Papers | Defend a claim | model, method, related work, evaluation, limitations |

Notes are allowed to be smaller and sharper. A note can say: "this boundary is
interesting", "this protocol behavior is fragile", or "this invariant is the
right one to formalize next". It does not need to pretend to be finished.

Projects should be stricter. A project page should answer:

- What question was being tested?
- What artifact exists?
- What would convince a skeptical reader?
- What is missing or intentionally out of scope?

Papers sit at the end of that path. They should not be a prettier version of a
project page; they should be the strongest claim the artifact can support.

## What an artifact should expose

The best project pages are not glamorous demos. They are readable workbenches.
They show enough structure that someone can audit the work without reverse
engineering the repository from scratch.

```yaml
artifact:
  question: "What behavior is being measured?"
  evidence:
    - traces
    - normalized dataset
    - reproducible command
  failure_modes:
    - sampling bias
    - parser assumptions
    - deployment drift
```

That shape is useful because it forces the page to separate claim from evidence.
If the claim is too broad, the artifact section will look thin. If the artifact
is strong, the page can stay quiet and still feel convincing.

```ts
type ResearchOutput = {
  claim: string;
  artifact: "note" | "project" | "paper";
  evidence: Array<"code" | "trace" | "model" | "measurement" | "review">;
  limitations: string[];
};
```

> The goal is not to make every page longer. The goal is to make every page
> answer the reader's next technical question.

## Why this matters

For PhD applications and research labs, the reader is often trying to infer fit:
what problems you notice, how you reduce them, and whether your work has enough
precision to survive scrutiny.

For companies, the signal is adjacent but different: can you turn ambiguity into
an artifact that other people can operate, inspect, and improve?

That is the standard this portfolio should optimize for.
